Visualizing the SAT


Rather helpfully, it turns out that the state of Connecticut publishes data on the participation and performance of the students at each of its high schools on the SAT. Using the SAT School Participation and Performance dataset in concert with Google Fusion Tables, we were able to map each Connecticut high school and color-code them by the proportion of students who scored a 1550 (out of 2400) or above, which the state considers to be an indicator of college and career readiness.




We then obtained income statistics for (almost) each county subdivision in Connecticut by scraping Wikipedia. By combining this dataset with geographical boundary data from the University of Connecticut, we were able to again use Google Fusion Tables to color-code the county subdivisions of Connecticut by their median family income.





We created an overlay of the two (as a static image, because our knowledge of how to use Google Fusion Tables only goes so far).



From this overlay, we're able to observe that at least to the human eye, the pockets of poorest performance on the SAT do seem to cluster within the county subdivisions in the lowest socioeconomic bracket; likewise, the schools with the highest performance on the SAT are all located within the two wealthiest county subdivisions, which report median family incomes in excess of $200,000 a year.

Although the trend is easiest to observe in the poorest and richest populations, the relationship between socioeconomic status and SAT score is noticeable in the middle groups as well. A couple of interesting observations: only three schools where more than 60% of students scored 1550 or above were located in county subdivisions with median family incomes under $100,000 a year; and not a single school in a county subdivision with a median family income over $100,000 a year had fewer than 40% of its students score 1550 or above.
